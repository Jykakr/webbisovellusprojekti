About Me

My name is Jyri Rosle-Krekil√§. I am a bowyer. I make traditional wooden bows. I also make arrows and other traditional archery equipment. I have been making bows for about 6 years now. I have made about 20 bows, I wish the number being higher, but I have been busy with other things. I have made bows from different woods, ash, maple, beech, service berry, bird cherry, bamboo and bamboo backed jatoba. I specialize in making longbows, but I have made a few recurves too. I also make arrows and strings as well as I have made Mary Rose style arrow and bow bags.

I have been researching bow desings and construction almost daily, in order to improve on my bows performance. I love finding out every bit of knowledge I can, about how every little detail affects the bows performance. I started first with English longbows, because I was given the impression, that they are the best bows. Then I found out it wasn't so, and I started to almost despite them because I felt betrayed and lied to, and I turned out to be a fan of asiatic "horse bow" designs.

That's when I started the interest of finding out what excactly makes them better, all I heard from every one was that the ridgid "ears", the siyahs, make the bow faster because of leverage. It wasn't enough explanation for me, so I started to research it. I found out what affect the leverage has on the bow. I found out that if the siyah has forward angle, it will make the bow as a shorter bow, until the bow is drawn to the point where the string angle to the siyah starts to open up, when the siyah starts to offer lever assistance, the bow will start to act as a longer bow. As we know, any bendy obyect will be stiffer if shortened and vise versa. So the bow will be stiffer at the beginning of the draw and will get more flexible as the draw progresses. This causes the bow to store more energy at the beginning of the draw than same draw weight straight bow. This made me believe that asiatic bows are always better, but I have since found out that the truth is not so simple.

Longbow, not as simple as you taught

First I would like to point out that the word longbow, refers to multiple types of bows, there i no one single type of longbow. Most of the text here is about English longbow and similar, but same laws of physics apply to all types of bows.

Back to the topic. After I made more research on longbows, I found out that they are not as simple as I thought. I taught that they are supposed to form perfect part of a circle while drawn, but I found out that they are not. I found out that the best longbows are not perfectly round, but they have elliptical tiller (the way the bow bends), they are thicker in the middle and thinner at the ends. That means the bend should not be circular, because the the thicker wood in the middle can not bend as much as the thinner wood at the ends without taking set (permanent bend caused by minor compression damage). Set worsens the bows ability to react to changes, it also makes the bow weaker. So for the same draw weight, the bow needs to be thicker, which in terms means heavier, and as such slower, thanks to the extra mass the stored energy has to move around. Not only that but the set also usually causes the bow to have string follow (meaning the tips are behind the handle when unstrung), which means the bow has less pretension, hence less early draw weight (stored energy).

Additionally the elliptical tiller makes the bow more efficient, because the stored energy is more focused closer to the tips, which means it has less limb lenght to move around, and the tips can be lighter, hence faster. The elliptical tiller also makes the bow more evenly stressed, which reduces the risk of excess set. It is often said that longbows are slow, because they don't have leverage, but leverage it self is not actually that beneficial to the bow. The bow as a mechanical devise, cannot generate more energy than what is stored into it. The leverage actually makes the tips and thus the string more difficult to move. Think of it this way, if you take a heavy hammer, it takes less effort to swing it if you hold it near the head, than at the end of the handle, it is the same for the bow, the leverage is actually fighting against the stored energy. The leverage is only beneficial if the lever is actually lighter than the bending limb. This is also confirmed in the traditional bowyers bible series, as it also states that mollegabet bow (type of longbow that has long levers) is not faster than conventional bow, unless the levers are lighter.

One might ask then why does the asiatic bows have levers, if they are not beneficial? The answer is that they are not wooden bows, but composites of horn, wood and sinew, which in fact is heavier material than wood alone, so the wooden siyah is actually lighter than the limb would be if made from the composite material to the tip. Additionally, making siyahs from just wood, allows the use of smaller horns to make the bow, increasing the range of suitable horn sizes for bow construction, especially in areas where large horned animals are not available.

Overall, what straight longbow looses in stored energy, compared to asiatic bows, it saves in moving mass, which eavens out the field, and makes the bows perform surprisingly similarly. On average, asiatic bows are faster, but not neserrily because they are better, but because they are often made better, possibly because not many people actually realise how to make a good longbow. I fact, asiatic bows that doesn't have forward angle in the siyahs, perform often the same, sometimes even worse than longbows, depending on the build quality of the bows. The asiatic bows that have forward angle in the siyahs, have the potential of being faster, but again, it depends on the build quality. Forward angle siyah bows are also more difficult to make, because the forward angle brings instability and the bow can actually unstring itself if become twisted. Backward angled siyahs, so called non contact siyahs, are more stable, but with the cost of lacking the higher energy storage potential.

So how fast can a longbow be? Well, I have made a longbow that is 71" long, 51# at 28" draw, and it shoots 183 fps with 530 grain arrows. That is pretty fast for any traditional bow, even for a commercial modern material recurve. Usually the commercial bows that reaches those speeds are either super recurves, or asiatic bows with much longer draw lenght than the 28" standard. The bow I made is not even a perfect example of what a longbow can be at its best, because it is made to be similar to "viking" bows, for example Hedeby (or Haithabu) bow, which is a bow found from, and named after a viking age settlement in Denmark. My bow is fire hardened and has a flat belly, which makes it better at compression than the "viking" bows, but still not the most ideal longbow. The best historical longbow desing in my opinion is Norwegian military style bow, which is like elongated paddle bow, with short wide limbs and long narrow handle. The handle also bends some and the lenght of it adds draw lenght, the wide limbs allow for more bend close to the tips, which was the more efficient location for it, as mentioned earlier. The bow also includes narrow and light tips, for low moving mass. Bows like this have allegebly been able to surpass 200 fps with 10 gpp arrows. I have not made a bow like this yet, but I am planning to make one (hopefully several) in the future. Have to be added tough, that those bows were laminated, so they were not made from single piece of wood, but from compression pine backed with birch or elm. This gives the bow more advance over simple self bow, and bows made from different desings and different materials have also allegebly been able to reach over 200 fps. I use the word allegebly, because I have not seen any of those bows in person, and I have not seen any chronograph videos about them, so I have to take the word of the people who made them.
